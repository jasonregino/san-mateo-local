Gone But Not Forgotten

San Mateo isn't only what's here now. It's also what we lost. A small tribute to the places that closed their doors but never quite left. Remember one we're missing? Tell us, and we'll add it.

Do you remember these? Help us confirm →

1934 – 2008

Bay Meadows Racetrack

S Delaware St (now the Bay Meadows neighborhood)

For nearly seventy-five years, Bay Meadows was San Mateo's grandstand. It opened in 1934 as California's premier thoroughbred track, the only major West Coast course to run straight through World War II, and the place where Seabiscuit won back-to-back handicaps in 1937 and 1938. It gave racing the photo-finish camera and the enclosed electric starting gate before the rest of the sport caught up. But a lot of locals remember it as a family day out as much as a racetrack: while the parents watched from the grandstands, the kids would head through the tunnel under the track to the playground out on the infield, and some even remember a few holes of golf out there. The last race was run on August 17, 2008.

The grounds live on as the Bay Meadows neighborhood beside the Hillsdale Caltrain station, where new parks and playgrounds keep the old ground a gathering place.

For decades · now closed

Shore-Vu Club

596 S Norfolk St, Shoreview

The neighborhood dive bar at the far end of the Shoreview strip on South Norfolk, the kind of place where the regulars knew each other and the bartender knew the regulars. Pool tables in the back, a bit of outdoor seating, and, going way back, its own plastic drink tokens stamped for a free round. It ran for decades as Club Shore-Vu before finally calling last call.

Drank a round at the Shore-Vu, or remember when it opened and closed? Send us your story and we'll fill in the years.

Purity → Villa Foods → Key Market → Chavez

Key Market

Shoreview Shopping Center, S Norfolk St

The grocery that anchored the Shoreview Shopping Center through a whole run of names. Old-timers knew it as Purity Market back in the 1960s, then Villa Foods through the seventies into the early eighties, and for a lot of people it will always be Key Market. Whatever the sign said, the deli counter was the draw. The sandwiches were the stuff of legend for the kids who grew up on Patricia, Fallon, and Dakota. The space still sells groceries today as Chavez Market.

The corner still feeds the neighborhood, but a lot of San Mateo grew up on Key Market's deli.

Now condos

All American Market

S Norfolk St, across from Shoreview Shopping Center

The neighborhood grocery that stood just across Norfolk Street from the Shoreview Shopping Center, a second market within sight of the first. Locals shopped it for years before it closed, and the lot where it stood is residential condos now.

Remember All American Market, the years and what you'd pick up there? Send it our way.

Now closed

Chicken Unlimited

9th Ave & B St, Downtown

The fried-chicken spot at the corner of 9th and B that fed a generation of San Mateans before it closed. The written record is thin, but the name still rings a bell for anyone who ate there.

Remember Chicken Unlimited and the years it ran? Send us your memories and we'll fill it in.

A Coyote Point rite of passage

Magic Mountain Playground

Coyote Point Recreation Area

The infamous concrete playground at Coyote Point, equal parts wonderland and hazard. Its claim to fame was a set of long concrete slides that kids rode down on flattened cardboard boxes, and the daredevils swore that tossing a handful of sand down first made you go faster. A whole generation of scraped knees and giant grins came off that hill.

If you rode those slides, you already know. Share your cardboard-and-sand stories.

1964 – 1997

The Castaway Restaurant

Coyote Point

The special-occasion restaurant perched at Coyote Point, with wide views out over the bay and a tropical, tiki-era feel. For more than thirty years it was where San Mateo went to celebrate: anniversaries, graduations, prom dinners, a nice night out by the water. It served its last meal in 1997.

Celebrated something at the Castaway? Tell us the occasion.

A downtown cornerstone · into the 1980s

Levy Brothers

4th Ave & Ellsworth, Downtown

For generations, Levy Brothers was the department store of downtown San Mateo, a Peninsula institution on the corner of 4th and Ellsworth. It was where families went for clothes, gifts, and the whole downtown shopping ritual before the malls pulled everyone away. By the mid-1980s it had closed, and downtown lost one of its anchors.

Shopped at Levy's? Tell us the exact year it closed and what you'd go there for.

Now a furniture store

Hillsdale Cinema

Across from Hillsdale Shopping Center

The movie theater that sat right across from the Hillsdale mall, where a lot of San Mateo teenagers spent their weekends. The screens are long gone and the building is a furniture store now, but plenty of first dates and Friday nights happened under that marquee.

What's the last movie you caught at the Hillsdale? Send it our way.

Now closed

QFI Market

San Mateo

A San Mateo neighborhood grocery that has since closed its doors. The written record is thin, but the name stuck with the locals who shopped it.

Remember QFI, where it stood and what years it ran? Fill us in and we'll tell it right.

Founded 1880s · a hometown daily for generations

San Mateo Times

San Mateo County

For over a century, the San Mateo Times was the paper that landed on Peninsula porches: the local record of the county, the high-school scores, the city-council fights, the obituaries, the department-store ads. Founded in the 1880s, it was San Mateo's hometown daily until it was folded into the larger Bay Area newspaper groups and the San Mateo Times name slipped off the masthead.

The Times told San Mateo's story for generations. Now it's part of ours.

Downtown movie house · gone

San Mateo Theater

66 3rd Ave, Downtown

The downtown movie house on 3rd Avenue whose marquee was iconic enough to turn up behind Ronald Reagan in a 1966 photo. Kids walked over on their own for ten-cent second-run summer matinees, Annette and Frankie beach movies, and the occasional B horror flick. The London Shop sold men's clothes right next door.

Catch a matinee at the San Mateo Theater? Tell us what was playing.

1981 – 1995

Fashion Island Mann 6

Fashion Island Blvd

The six-screen cinema at the old Fashion Island mall, where a generation caught their movies, some of them paying for one and quietly slipping into another. The site is part of the Bridgepointe parking lot today.

Snuck from one screen to another at the Mann 6? We won't tell.

Drive-in decades · gone

A&W / Foster's Freeze

El Camino Real, near 27th Ave

The drive-in on El Camino across from Ellingson's, and a whole era of cruising. Carhops on roller skates, root beer floats in frosted mugs, the Papa Burger and the Momma Burger, and strawberry shakes with actual pieces of strawberry. When the Foster's Freeze days ended the owner grilled free steaks to send it off. The corner later became Carozzi's, and then Taxi's with a classic cab parked out front.

Cruised to the A&W? Tell us your order.

24 hours · gone

Happy Donuts

El Camino Real, just north of 12th Ave

The 24-hour donut shop on El Camino, home of the legendary devil-cake donut and a beacon for anyone out too late or up too early. When it finally closed, the neighborhood loved that big sign enough to rescue and keep it.

Devil cake at two in the morning? You know the one.

El Camino landmark · gone

Lyon's Restaurant

El Camino Real

The landmark coffee-shop-and-diner on El Camino, right across from the old Pacific recording studio. The kind of all-day booth-and-counter spot the Peninsula had on every other block before they quietly disappeared.

Had a regular booth at Lyon's? Send us the memory.

1960s–70s · gone

Shoreview Variety Store

Shoreview Shopping Center

The five-and-dime in the Shoreview center, with the coin-operated horse out front that every kid begged for a ride on, and penny and nickel candy inside. There was even a little post office and a barber shop tucked in the back.

Rode the horse out front? So did we.

"The big E" · gone

The Emporium

Hillsdale Shopping Center

The Emporium, the big E, one of the Hillsdale anchors and a back-to-school pilgrimage for a couple of generations of San Mateo families. New hires went through famously intense training. The chain folded in the mid-1990s and the name went with it.

Did your back-to-school shopping at the big E? So did everyone.

Record-store days · gone

Tower Records

San Mateo

Back when buying music meant flipping through bins for a whole afternoon, Tower Records was the spot. One local even met Monty Python's Terry Jones there signing a book. The chain is long gone now, but the memory of that yellow-and-red sign isn't.

First album you bought at Tower? Tell us.

Pre-Foster City · long gone

Seal Beach

the old San Mateo waterfront

Before Foster City filled in the marsh, San Mateo kids swam at Seal Beach on the bay, where a submarine-shaped playground structure sat right at the water's edge. The water was slimy and nobody cared. It has all been built over now, but plenty of people learned to swim out there.

Played on that submarine? Share the memory.

Family restaurant · gone

The Cinnamon Mill

Delaware St, near the Ross center

A family restaurant in the Delaware Street building near the Ross shopping center, the kind of place people fondly try to recall by name years later. The space later became a Pantry.

Remember the Cinnamon Mill's menu? Fill us in.

Hillsdale anchor · gone

Mervyn's

Hillsdale Shopping Center

For a couple of decades Mervyn's at Hillsdale was where San Mateo did back-to-school: the wall of Levi's 501s, the racks of acid-washed denim, the affordable everything. It anchored the mall until the chain wound down and the store closed for good.

Back-to-school haul from Mervyn's? We all had one.

1980s–90s · now another bar

Yuppie Bar

709 South B St

The South B Street bar known for strong, cheap drinks, a carpeted room, free ping pong, and a cast of local legends. The address had been Bucky's, Arnie's, and Joe's in earlier lives, and it is a different bar again today, but the Yuppie years have their own following.

Closed out a night at the Yuppie? Tell us a story, a printable one.

Now closed

TGIF

across from the mall, near the train station

The spot across from the mall near the train station that locals swear had the best chicken strips around, crispy, with honey mustard for dipping and a pile of fries. It's long gone, but that order still sticks in people's memory.

Remember TGIF's chicken strips? Tell us the years it was around.

Now closed

Stop N Go

San Mateo

The old Stop N Go, one of those neighborhood convenience stores that always seemed to be there for a soda, a snack, or a quick run, until it wasn't.

Remember which corner the Stop N Go was on? Send it our way.

1988 – 2024

Sushi Sam's Edomata

218 E 3rd Ave, Downtown

For thirty-six years this was the Peninsula's sushi landmark, the counter people drove across the Bay for. It was famously one of Mark Zuckerberg's favorites; he donated a hundred thousand dollars to help it through the pandemic. Chef-owner Sam Sugiyama served his last plate in December 2024 and retired.

The legacy lives on: Sam's family reopened as Sushi Edomata on 25th Avenue, with his nephew behind the counter.

2018 – 2025

Wursthall

310 Baldwin Ave, Downtown

The German beer hall from food writer Kenji López-Alt, with house-made sausages and more than twenty-five beers on tap. It put downtown San Mateo on a lot of food-world maps before quietly closing in September 2025.

Its downstairs cocktail bar, Wunderbar, is still pouring.

1990 – 2014

Ristorante Capellini

310 Baldwin Ave, Downtown

The upscale Italian spot on B and Baldwin that a lot of people credit with kick-starting downtown San Mateo's whole restaurant scene. Aaron Ferer opened it in 1990 because he was tired of everyone driving up to the city for a nice dinner. It ran twenty-four years, some of the staff nearly the whole time, before closing in 2014.

The same Baldwin Avenue corner later became Wursthall, right above.

1953 – 2020

Talbot's Toyland

B Street & 5th Ave, Downtown

Sixty-six years of San Mateo kids grew up in this toy store, with the bike shop, Talbot's Cyclery, right alongside it. Family-run for generations, it kept expanding into neighboring storefronts until it filled a whole stretch of B Street. When it closed in 2020, it took a piece of four generations' childhoods with it.

1972 – 2019

Trag's Market

303 Baldwin Ave, Downtown

The Tragoutsis family's downtown grocery, the corner market that downtown actually had. After forty-seven years it closed in 2019, and a lot of neighbors felt the loss of a real grocery store you could walk to.

1982 – 2023

The Fish Market

1855 S Norfolk St

For about forty-one years this was the Peninsula's seafood go-to: the oyster bar, the mesquite grill, the fresh catch on ice. The San Mateo location closed in 2023, ending the chain's long run in the Bay Area.

The brand still operates down in Southern California.

1982 – 1996

San Mateo Fashion Island

Where Bridgepointe is now

The enclosed mall by the 92 and 101 interchange, with JCPenney, Bullock's, a food court, a multiplex, an indoor ice rink, and the Gold Mine (later Tilt) arcade where half of San Mateo blew their allowance as kids. It struggled almost from the start and closed as a mall in 1996, demolished a few years later. The ice rink, somehow, outlived it all the way to 2013.

The site is the open-air Bridgepointe Shopping Center today. And who else remembers that arcade?

Closed around 2020

B Street Station

236 S B St, Downtown

A dim, easygoing downtown cocktail spot, Edison bulbs and big communal tables, the kind of place you ended up at without planning to. It closed around 2020.

2007 – 2016

38th Floor Bar

38 E 25th Ave

It opened as the Mandala Lounge and got its odd name after a Bar Rescue TV makeover in 2015. The fame didn't save it; it closed about a year later, in 2016. A short, strange chapter, but a San Mateo one.

Bar 12

E 4th Ave

A neighborhood dive on 4th Avenue. The kind of place that doesn't leave much of a paper trail, just regulars who remember it. If you've got the story, we'd love to hear it.

Wolmer's Music

4th Ave & B, now a phone store

A longtime downtown music shop; the storefront is a T-Mobile now. We couldn't pin down all the dates, so if you remember Wolmer's, help us tell it right.

1950 – 1988

The Lanai

Inside the Villa Hotel, San Mateo

San Mateo's original tiki temple, tucked inside the old Villa Hotel for thirty-eight years. It went all in: a twenty-foot fish tank, a waterfall wall, an authentic luau pit, and a huge South Seas mural lit to shift from day to night, all under the glow of a neon palm tree out front. When it closed in 1988, its koa tables and tiki decor moved to a little strip-mall spot that didn't last the decade. The real thing, gone but never quite forgotten.

1950s – tower demolished 2001

The Hillsdale Inn Tower

Along Highway 101, San Mateo

The Hillsdale Inn was a Highway 101 hot spot, and its showpiece was a tower shaped like an air-traffic-control tower, a honeymoon suite floating in the sky. Couples booked the suite-in-the-sky for years, but it sat unused after the mid-1970s and slowly fell apart. Deemed structurally unsafe, the tower came down in April 2001. Some called it a landmark, some called it an eyesore; either way, the skyline lost something.

Closed around 2021

The Downtown Tiki Lounge

144 S B St, Downtown

A proper tiki bar in the middle of downtown, thatched roofs, dim booths, and rum drinks with little umbrellas. When it closed, the space was reborn as Fogbird, the bright cocktail lounge there now, so the address carries on even though the tiki torches went out.

San Mateo tiki lore runs in a line: The Lanai, then this, and now Fogbird on the same block.

Its castle years, until 1997

The Dunfey Hotel

1770 S Amphlett Blvd

Long before it was the San Mateo Marriott, this hotel looked like a Tudor castle. It started life as the Royal Coach Inn, all turrets and old-world theming, and later became the Dunfey Hotel. A 1997 remodel traded the castle for a Spanish-villa look, so the building still stands, but the castle a lot of us grew up driving past is gone.

Closed around 2025

M & H Market (aka Fred's Market)

159 N Kingston St

The corner neighborhood market on Kingston, known to different generations as M & H Market and as Fred's, run for years by Henry Herro. Fresh Mexican bread delivered like clockwork, a candy counter the kids remember, and the everyday stuff the block ran on. The kind of little store a neighborhood doesn't realize it needs until it's gone.

Long since closed

Telecenter

1830 S Delaware St

The go-to San Mateo spot for appliances, TVs, and electronics, back when you bought that stuff in person from people who knew your name. Long gone now, but ask around and someone will still finish the slogan for you.

"If it's not from Telecenter, you paid too much."

1991 – 2016

Tres Amigos

243 S B St, Downtown

A downtown taqueria that fed B Street for more than twenty-five years before it lost its lease and served its last tacos on Christmas Day, 2016. One of the anchors of the old B Street 'amigos' lineup, missed by everyone who had a regular order there.

1949 – 2005

The Palm Theatre

1705 Palm Ave

It opened in 1949 as a proper neighborhood movie house, was briefly the Paris Theater, and then spent its last thirty years as San Mateo's one and only adults-only cinema (we'll leave it there). It came down in 2005 and condos went up, though a few of the Palm's etched-glass panels were saved and tucked into the new building's rec room.

1947 – 2001

The Rolladium

863 Amphlett Blvd, by Highway 101

The Peninsula's roller rink, right off 101, where generations learned to skate and blew out birthday candles. Its maple floor was a survivor, salvaged from an earlier rink that burned in the 1940s and skated on for something like ninety years. The wheels stopped for good in 2001.

The Broiler

1855 S Delaware St

By local memory, the restaurant that held the South Delaware spot now home to The Pantry. We couldn't dig up the dates, so if you ate at The Broiler, help us fill in its story.

19th Avenue Bowl

San Mateo

A mid-century San Mateo bowling alley, remembered on old matchbooks and by anyone who rolled a home game there. It's long gone, and the exact address and years have gotten foggy, so if you remember 19th Avenue Bowl, set us straight.

2000 – 2018

Kingfish

201 S B St (the House of Merkel), Downtown

The seafood restaurant and bar that was the last tenant of the historic House of Merkel at Morse's Corner, a building that had been, over the decades, a cigar store, a tailor, an antique shop, Redbird Brewery, and Barley & Hopps before it. Kingfish closed in 2018 after about eighteen years; the building sold for over ten million and holds offices now.

Gone by 2006

The Good Guys

41 W Hillsdale Blvd

The Bay Area electronics chain half of us bought our first stereo or TV from. The San Mateo store started out on El Camino before moving to Hillsdale Boulevard, right by Tower Records, where the two eventually shared a roof as a 'WOW!' superstore. When the whole chain was sold off, the stores closed by 2006, taking a piece of Hillsdale, and Tower Records, down with them.

Closed in recent years

Byron's Shoes

101 E 4th Ave, Downtown

The downtown shoe store where San Mateo got fitted for years, run by Byron Eisenberg. It's gone now, though fittingly another shoe shop, Footwear etc., stepped into the same 4th Avenue spot.

Closed 2023

San Mateo Electronics

16 W 42nd Ave

The old-school electronics and parts shop, the place you went for a fuse, a cable, an odd little component, and advice from someone who actually knew what they were talking about. When it finally closed, the Daily Journal called it the end of an era, because it was.

1941 – 1982

Manor Theater

25th Ave

The two-screen neighborhood theater on 25th Avenue, seating around 800, where a lot of San Mateo did its Saturday matinees and date nights. It ran from 1941 to 1982; the original building still stands, converted to offices.

Saw a movie at the Manor? Tell us what was on the marquee.

1931 – 1950s

Baywood Theater

B Street

A grand single-screen house on B Street that seated over a thousand in its day. It ran from 1931 into the 1950s before the space became a Thrifty and, later, retail.

Remember the Baywood? We'd love the details.

1915 – 1933

Regent Theater

San Mateo Dr & Baldwin

One of San Mateo's earliest movie houses, a 600-seat single screen at San Mateo Drive and Baldwin. It ran from 1915 to 1933, later served as a Fox West Coast warehouse, and is offices today.

A silent-era memory of the Regent? Share it.

1915 – 1918

Hart's Theater

B Street

One of the very first movie theaters in town, a 350-seat house on B Street that ran only from 1915 to 1918. The Attic Restaurant later took the spot.

Know more about Hart's? Help us fill it in.

1953 – 1954

Studio Drive-In

Peninsula Ave

A short-lived drive-in on Peninsula Avenue with room for 500 cars and a novel enclosed glass viewing room. Noise complaints shut it down almost as fast as it opened.

Caught a show at the Studio? Rare memory, send it.

1950 – 1951

19th Ave Drive-In

Grant & 19th

The drive-in at Grant and 19th that could pack in up to 770 cars for its brief run around 1950. The lot later became a Payless, then Rite Aid and a Handy Man, and it is housing today.

Remember the 19th Ave Drive-In? Tell us.

1943 – 1988

Vince's Italian & Seafood

16th & El Camino Real

For forty-five years Vince's was the family Italian-and-seafood spot at 16th and El Camino, with Mrs. Licata greeting guests, a massive swordfish mounted on the wall, and a Shirley Temple that kids still remember. It served its last plate in 1988.

Dinner at Vince's? Tell us your table order.

1970s–80s

The Magic Pan

Hillsdale Shopping Center

The creperie at Hillsdale famous for its spinach-souffle crepe, the monte cristo, the chantilly crepe, and a salad with mandarin oranges and almonds. A lot of birthday lunches happened in those booths before the chain faded away.

Order a crepe at the Magic Pan? Which one?

1956 – 2010s

Esposto's Delicatessen

Borel Square / South B St

The delicatessen and caterer that fed San Mateo weddings and events for over half a century, a South B Street go-to. It closed after a landlord raised the rent, though the family has since resurfaced out by the airport.

Esposto's catered your wedding? We'd love the story.

1970s–90s

Santini's Pizza

2214 El Camino Real, near 25th

Widely sworn to be the best pizza San Mateo ever had, everything made from scratch at 2214 El Camino. People still say they can taste it just thinking about it. Long gone, and never quite replaced.

Was Santini's really the best? Settle it for us.

1947 – 1984

Ellingson's Sporting Goods

El Camino Real (later 27th Ave)

The family-owned hunting, fishing, and outdoor store that a generation of kids remembers most for the taxidermy: buffalo heads, a pilot bear in a glass case, and a giant Kodiak bear that terrified the little ones. It is where you got your first baseball glove or WristRocket, from 1947 until it closed in 1984.

Scared of the big bear at Ellingson's? You weren't alone.

Downtown anchor · gone

Roos Bros / Roos Atkins

4th Ave, Downtown

A downtown 4th Avenue clothing anchor and many a teenager's first job, back before it became Roos Atkins and, eventually, history. Part of the old run of 4th Ave shops alongside Woolworth and Blum's.

First job at Roos? Tell us about it.

Five-and-dime · gone

F.W. Woolworth

4th Ave, Downtown

The classic downtown five-and-dime on 4th Avenue: the lunch counter, the candy, a little bit of everything. Woolworth shuttered its stores nationwide in the 1990s, and downtown lost a fixture.

Lunch at the Woolworth counter? We remember.

1970s–80s

Pacific Stereo

1114 S El Camino Real

The place a lot of San Mateo bought its first home and car stereo, at 1114 South El Camino, in the building that had been the Gaslight before it. The chain is long gone now.

Bought your first stereo at Pacific? Tell us the setup.

Local clothier · gone

Schneider's

San Mateo (now a Peet's)

A well-known local clothier carrying apparel for men, boys, and ladies, often mentioned in the same breath as Levy Brothers. The spot pours coffee as a Peet's today.

Shopped Schneider's? Tell us what you'd pick up.

Late 1960s–70s

Unimart (Parkview Gem)

Grant Ave near 101 & 19th

A monster-size discount department store off Grant near the freeway, once known as Parkview Gem, where Santa famously arrived by helicopter into the parking lot at Christmas. The site is the Cross Winds office park and YMCA now.

See Santa's helicopter land at Unimart? Best memory.

1960s · now condos

Shop Rite Market

5th & B St, Downtown

The downtown market at the corner of 5th and B, run by the same family behind Zorba's. The site is condos today.

Remember Shop Rite? Fill in the years for us.

Beloved family market · gone

Petrini's Market

Hillsdale & 42nd Ave

A great family market with the motto Where Shopping is a Pleasure, where more than a few locals recall relatives working the meat department. Gone now, but fondly remembered.

Family work at Petrini's? We'd love to hear it.

Founded 1968 · now a spa store

Pacific Recording Studio

1737 S El Camino Real

The Bay Area's first 16-track recording studio, opened in 1968 at 1737 South El Camino, and a genuine piece of music history: Santana, the Grateful Dead, the Doobie Brothers, and Van Morrison all recorded here. Lyon's diner sat right across El Camino. The building is a spa-supply store today.

Knew someone who recorded at Pacific? We want that story.
