Marlowe & Vine: The Corner Bakery That Runs on Regulars

Updated July 2026

How a small downtown coffee-and-pastry corner became a morning ritual for half the neighborhood, one sourdough loaf and first-name hello at a time.

This is a sample. Marlowe & Vine is a made-up business we created to show exactly what a Business Spotlight looks like. Yours would be written from your own answers, in your own voice.

Marlowe & Vine started, like a lot of good things, with someone leaving a job they were good at to do something they loved more. Nora Ellison spent a decade in restaurant kitchens before she signed a lease on a narrow corner spot on B Street in 2019. She wanted a place where the neighborhood could slow down for ten minutes, a good cup of coffee, a warm pastry, and a face behind the counter that knew your name.

The menu stays small on purpose. Sourdough is mixed the night before and baked in the dark hours, so the first loaves hit the shelf right as the door opens. There are laminated pastries that take three days to make and about ninety seconds to sell out, a rotating quiche, and single-origin coffee pulled slow. Nora would rather do six things beautifully than thirty things well enough.

What regulars come back for isn't only the food. It's the little pay-it-forward chalkboard by the register, where anyone can leave a coffee for a stranger who needs one. It's the fact that the baristas start your usual before you reach the counter. In a downtown that has changed storefronts a dozen times over, Marlowe & Vine has quietly become the kind of place people build their mornings around.

If you have never been in, come early. The croissants go fast, the light is best before nine, and there is almost always a loaf still warm enough to fog the paper bag on the walk home.

In their own words

How did your business get started here?

I cooked in restaurants for years and loved it, but I wanted something slower, something that belonged to a neighborhood. This corner on B Street had been empty for a while. I walked past it every day until I finally stopped talking myself out of it. We opened in 2019 with a tiny menu and a lot of hope.

What's one thing customers don't know about what you do?

How early the day starts. The sourdough gets mixed the night before and I am here in the dark, around four in the morning, to bake it off. By the time the first customer walks in for coffee, we have already put in half a day. People see the pretty pastry case, not the 4am part.

What's your favorite part of serving this community?

Knowing people by their order. There is a couple who have come in every Saturday since we opened, and their kid grew up in that time from a stroller to ordering her own cocoa. You do not get that working anywhere else. This corner has become a little bit of everyone's morning, and that still gets me.

What makes you different from the other cafes nearby?

We keep it small and we make almost everything ourselves. I would rather do six things beautifully than thirty things that are fine. If it is on our shelf, it came out of our oven that morning. That is the whole idea.

If someone's never been in, what should they expect?

Come before nine if you can. The light is lovely, the croissants are still warm, and it is quiet enough to actually sit for a minute. Get a coffee, get whatever looks best in the case, and say hi. We will remember you next time.
