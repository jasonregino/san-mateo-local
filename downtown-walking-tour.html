Self-Guided Walking Tour

Historic Downtown San Mateo

The town began right here, at B Street and Third Avenue. Take a slow walk past the century-old storefronts and here's what you're actually looking at.

San Mateo started as a Catholic mission outpost in the 1790s and a stage stop between San Francisco and San Jose. The railroad made it a town: by the 1880s, brick buildings lined the square around the station, and for decades South B Street was where everyone shopped, the pharmacies, meat markets, cigar stores, hardware and lumber.

When East Third Avenue filled in through the 1920s and 1930s, San Mateo boomed as a San Francisco suburb, and the downtown took on the Spanish Colonial and Art Deco look it still wears. When the malls arrived in the 1950s, the big stores drifted south to Hillsdale, and that quiet exodus is exactly what left downtown's 1930s character intact. Here are the stops worth slowing down for.

Building histories drawn from the City of San Mateo Planning Division's Downtown Historic Building Self-Guided Walking Tour. Most of these buildings still stand, look up above the storefronts.

Where to start: Park once and walk the whole thing. Downtown has several public parking garages and lots around B Street between Second and Fourth Avenues, plus parking by the Caltrain station and over near Central Park. Any of them drops you a block or two from the first stop. The route runs down South B Street, then loops back along East Third Avenue.

Along South B Street

The city's original main street.

1

The Wisnom Building

100 S B St · Built 1907

An early survivor in the Mission Revival style. Robert Wisnom, a giant of early San Mateo commerce and construction, ran a lumber yard behind this building starting in the 1880s. The Wisnom name is on storefronts all over downtown.

Find it →
2

Odd Fellows Hall

113 S B St · Built 1891

One of the oldest structures left on B Street, built for the Odd Fellows, the oldest fraternal organization in San Mateo. It also housed the San Mateo Market and the Mountain View Fruit Company. Note the richly detailed brick upper bays.

Find it →
3

Noah's Cafeteria (site)

139 S B St · Built 1900

In the 1920s this address was Noah's Cafeteria, run by one of the first known Black businessmen in San Mateo. Well known across the area with a growing clientele, Noah later moved his restaurant up to Third Avenue.

Find it →
4

The old bank building

164 S B St · Built 1925

An elegant Greek Revival building that cost $50,000, designed by architect W.H. Weeks and built by Samuel Wisnom, one of Robert's sons. A bank was the original occupant; look for the paired fluted pilasters.

Find it →
5

The House of Merkel

201 S B St · Built 1931

Considered by many the most significant historic building in San Mateo. German immigrants Carl and Cuna Merkel opened a cigar store on this corner in 1918, then built this Art Deco landmark and filled it with a soda fountain, confectionary, liquor store, billiard parlor, and horse betting. It was a gathering place for over fifty years. Look up at the octagonal corner bays and terra cotta tiling.

Find it →
6

Old City Hall

215 S B St · City Hall 1914 to 1961

This was San Mateo City Hall for nearly fifty years. The old judge's chamber still occupies the second floor, and a prison cell and a rifle range are tucked in the basement. The B Street frontage keeps its prominent ground-floor arches.

Find it →
7

The Woolworth building

240 S B St · Built 1937

A stylish Art Deco storefront that was home to the F.W. Woolworth five-and-dime in the late 1930s. Note the stepped vertical elements and the patterns sculpted into the stucco.

Find it →
8

The market building

250 S B St · Built 1926

Over the years this Spanish-mission-influenced building held the Tasty Cake Shop, a Piggly Wiggly, and a Safeway. One of its three storefronts still keeps its original transom window under the sloping clay-tile roof.

Find it →
9

National Bank of San Mateo

301 S B St · Built 1912

A reinforced-concrete Mission Revival building that housed the National Bank of San Mateo, San Mateo Hardware, and Pope's Drug Store in its first thirty years. It's one of the six San Mateo buildings on the National Register of Historic Places.

Find it →

Along East Third Avenue

The 1920s and 1930s suburban boom.

10

The Benjamin Franklin Hotel

36 E 3rd Ave · Built 1927

Eight stories, built for $250,000 as a resort for vacationing San Franciscans, designed by architect W.H. Weeks. The building still stands and still anchors the block, even as new businesses fill it.

Find it →
11

Levy Brothers

51 E 3rd Ave · Built 1931

An excellent Tudor-style building, decorative half-timbering, an elaborate chimney, scalloped bargeboards, a rare bit of storybook architecture downtown. For years this was the Levy Brothers store, a San Mateo retail institution.

Find it →
12

The old San Mateo Theatre

60 E 3rd Ave · Built 1924

Once a downtown movie theatre, later converted to offices. Its marquee and surface decoration were stripped away and a new front pushed out for ground-level shops, but the original upper portion is still visible above.

Find it →
13

J.J. Newberry

71 E 3rd Ave · Built 1931

The five-and-dime J.J. Newberry & Company moved in as the first tenant in 1935 and stayed until 1957, when it followed the shoppers south to the brand-new Hillsdale Mall, a small moment that captured the whole downtown-to-mall shift.

Find it →
14

Schneider's

208 E 3rd Ave · Built 1926

A simple modern building with Art Deco details at Third and Ellsworth. Its early tenant was the Greenline Drug Company; JCPenney moved in during the mid-1940s; and since the 1950s it has housed Schneider's, a locally owned apparel store. The tall Art Deco letters still spell the name below the cornice.

Find it →
15

The Medical Arts Building

205-221 E 3rd Ave · Built 1929

A handsome modernistic block with Art Deco ornament, one section four stories, one three. Chevrons decorate the spandrels between the piers, and the splayed main entrance keeps its original ornamental tile in stylized floral and geometric patterns.

Find it →
16

The Avansino Building

72 E 3rd Ave · Built 1936

San Francisco architects Fabre and Hildebrand designed this concrete-and-terra-cotta professional building for $28,000. Known for years simply as the Avansino Building, it has housed local businesses and offices since 1937.

Find it →
17

The Raybould & Johnson building

10 E 3rd Ave · Built 1924

An idiosyncratic two-part block with a ribbon of casement windows, a semi-elliptical surround, and a whimsical red-and-white checkerwork cornice. It was built for Raybould and Johnson, realtors who subdivided many of San Mateo's grand old estates.

Find it →

A few steps off the route

Worth the short detour.

18

Hotel St. Matthew

215-229 2nd Ave · Built 1900

Built as the Wisnom Hotel and renamed the Hotel St. Matthew in 1920, this handsome brick building is the only remaining downtown hotel of its era, with four octagonal window bays projecting above the ground floor. It's on the National Register of Historic Places.

Find it →
19

The old fire station

120 S Ellsworth Ave · Built 1939

This Spanish Colonial Revival fire station is all that remains of the 1940-era City Hall complex. Look for the hipped tile roof over all three sections and the lunettes arching over each door.

Find it →
